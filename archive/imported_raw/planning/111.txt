Positive-Geometry Research Pipeline — v2 Mission Identify stable computational regularities in positive-geometry-related objects quickly enough to infer what a native physical substrate would need to instantiate. This pipeline is not for paper-writing, autonomous theory-building, or metaphysical questions like “is positive geometry fundamental.” It is for extracting: • persistent invariants, • primitive operations, • scaling laws, • readout bottlenecks, • and domain-of-validity boundaries. Those are the outputs that constrain substrate design.   ⸻   Core design rules What small models are allowed to do Small models only do tasks with: • short context, • explicit grammars, • bounded search spaces, • hard external checks, • and cheap retry loops. They may: • propose candidates, • normalize encodings, • repair failed formal attempts, • classify against explicit criteria, • and translate between known representations. They may not be trusted to: • judge correctness, • maintain long-horizon research framing, • synthesize across a large literature, • invent formalisms, • or decide what matters physically. What tools are allowed to do External tools own: • symbolic truth, • combinatorial enumeration, • exact arithmetic, • SAT/SMT consistency, • and numeric stress-testing. What the human owns You own: • domain framing, • research priority, • substrate relevance, • interpretation of surviving structure, • and detection of pipeline self-deception. That division should never blur.   ⸻   Layer 0 — Structured Domain Memory This is the real center of the system. It is not just a cache. It is the research prior that constrains the proposer space and prevents the model from hallucinating a fake field. Contents Store everything as structured records, not prose-first notes. Each record should include: • object family, • parameters, • representation(s), • exact computed quantities, • invariants observed, • known identities, • triangulations / sign-flip data / boundary data, • known failures, • provenance, • confidence level, • applicability domain, • and links to derived artifacts. Required record types 1. Canonical examples Small trusted cases from literature or exact computation. 2. Counterexamples / failed conjectures These are as important as successes. 3. Representation maps Known equivalences between encodings. 4. Open tasks Explicit unresolved questions tied to data already present. 5. Verification recipes For each object family: what checks are meaningful. Additional requirement Every record needs: • source_type: literature / exact computation / numeric only / conjectural • trust_level: high / medium / low • scope: where it applies • failure_modes: known ways it breaks Without that metadata, later layers will overgeneralize.   ⸻   Layer 1 — Ground-Truth Generator Pure code. No LLM. Its job is to produce exact or high-confidence data for downstream search. Functions Depending on the family under study: • enumerate small positive-geometric objects, • compute boundary lattices and incidence relations, • compute canonical forms or approximations, • generate cosmohedron-like combinatorial structures, • compute residues, face counts, volumes, or related observables, • generate paired encodings of the same object, • and produce perturbations / near-miss variants. Output standard Every output must be emitted as a structured record: • input parameters, • generator version, • representation used, • result, • computation mode, • precision / exactness, • and cost. Critical rule Layer 1 must also generate negative and mutated cases, not just canonical ones. If you only generate beautiful known examples, the pipeline will learn the encoding style rather than the invariant.   ⸻   Layer 2A — Normalizer / Compression Proposer Small model or deterministic code. This layer should exist separately from conjecture generation. Its job is to convert raw outputs into stable, comparable forms. Tasks • canonicalize encodings, • extract explicit features, • label symmetries, • compress objects into normalized summaries, • translate between known equivalent representations, • and generate candidate invariant descriptors. Output Strict grammar only. For example: • normalized boundary list, • feature vector, • symmetry signature, • residue profile, • candidate invariant schema. Why this split matters You do not want the same model trying to both: 1. understand a messy object, and 2. invent a conjecture about it. That creates too much hidden error.   ⸻   Layer 2B — Conjecture Proposer Small model only. This model sees normalized objects, not raw ones. Allowed tasks • propose recurrence relations, • propose equivalence hypotheses, • propose invariant candidates, • propose extrapolation laws, • propose completion of partial structures, • propose minimal primitive sets, • repair failed formal attempts using explicit checker feedback. Constraints • output only in strict DSLs, • no prose outputs, • parse failure causes immediate rejection, • every call is retrieval-augmented from Layer 0, • every proposal tagged with intended object family and scope. Scoring target The proposer is optimized for: • novelty, • diversity, • and checkability. Not elegance.   ⸻   Layer 3 — Multi-View Checker This is where epistemic grounding lives. Truth never comes from the model. Required checking modes A conjecture should be tested through at least two independent views when possible: • symbolic check: exact algebraic consistency, • combinatorial check: incidence / count / structural consistency, • numeric check: high-precision agreement, • logical check: SAT/SMT feasibility or equivalence, • formal check: optional, for critical survivors. Output Each proposal gets: • pass/fail by check type, • failure trace, • scope of validity observed, • computational cost, • and unresolved ambiguity flags. Critical rule A conjecture that passes only one weak check is not a survivor. The architecture should explicitly distinguish: • weak survivor, • multi-view survivor, • formalized survivor.   ⸻   Layer 4 — Search Controller Pure code. Its job is to allocate attention. A pass/fail memory is not enough. It must track search quality. It should score proposals on: • success rate, • novelty relative to previous conjectures, • distance from known failures, • domain coverage, • checker diversity passed, • mutation robustness, • and substrate relevance potential. Substrate relevance potential This is a ranking proxy, not a truth claim. A conjecture scores higher if it appears likely to bear on: • primitive operations, • preserved structure, • scaling advantage, • readout constraints, • or native state representation. That keeps the loop from drifting into mathematically interesting but wedge-irrelevant territory.   ⸻   Layer 5 — Stress Harness Pure code. This is the anti-self-deception layer. Standard tests 1. Held-out scaling Test larger n, higher order, or new family members not shown during proposal. 2. Cross-family tests Apply conjecture to related but distinct cases. 3. Symmetry-preserving perturbations Change representation without changing the object. 4. Symmetry-breaking mutations Break a condition that should matter and confirm the conjecture fails. 5. Near-miss adversarial cases Generate objects that look similar under surface statistics but should invalidate the rule. Output Each surviving conjecture gets a robustness profile: • passed domains, • failed domains, • mutation sensitivity, • likely overfit risk, • extrapolation confidence. A conjecture that only survives “larger held-out cases” but fails mutation tests is not stable enough for substrate translation.   ⸻   Layer 6 — Substrate Translation Schema Do not jump directly from math to prose. Insert a formal intermediate schema first. Required schema fields For each strong survivor, record: • object_class • invariant_or_law • preservation_domain • failure_domain • evidence_type • scaling_behavior • primitive_operations_implied • representation_dependence • readout_implication • resource_implication • candidate_substrate_constraint • confidence_level • open ambiguity Example translation pattern Instead of: “Looks like this might matter for substrate design” You want: • invariant: boundary-adjacency preservation • evidence: multi-view + mutation-stable across family F • implication: native substrate must preserve adjacency structurally • failure mode: disappears under representation-changing postprocessing • readout implication: benefit lost if adjacency must be reconstructed classically Only after filling this schema should human-readable substrate statements be emitted.   ⸻   Layer 7 — Human Interpretation Layer This is where your scarce judgment should be spent. Not on babysitting the proposer. On selecting which survivors actually constrain physics. Your responsibilities • decide which schema entries are substrate-relevant, • distinguish structural invariants from encoding artifacts, • update the substrate spec, • redefine priorities, • kill dead directions, • and patch missing checks when false positives slip through. Review cadence Weekly • review strongest new survivors, • inspect repeated failures, • patch obvious checker blind spots. Biweekly • operational audit: • is the KB drifting? • is proposer diversity collapsing? • are checkers too narrow? • is review load growing too fast? Monthly • update substrate-spec document, • decide which families deserve more generator budget. Quarterly • strategic kill review: • has the system produced substrate-relevant output? • or is it just doing decorative positive-geometry research? If no substrate-relevant outputs emerge after a full quarter, something structural is wrong.   ⸻   What success looks like A useful output is not “interesting conjecture.” A useful output looks like: • “Operation P appears in every stable transition description tested; substrate must support P natively.” • “Invariant X is representation-stable and mutation-robust; substrate must preserve X structurally.” • “Classical cost scales like f(n), but geometric description keeps resource Q bounded across family F; investigate whether Q is the native state variable.” • “Readout destroys the apparent advantage unless observable R is emitted directly.” That is the wedge.   ⸻   Minimum viable v2 Do not build the whole stack at once. MVP target Pick one narrow family and one narrow question. Example: recover and stress-test recurrence laws or invariant candidates for a small positive-Grassmannian-derived family. Minimal components • Layer 0: 20–50 trusted records, including failed conjectures • Layer 1: one exact generator • Layer 2A: deterministic canonicalizer or tiny model • Layer 2B: one 7B proposer • Layer 3: symbolic + combinatorial checks • Layer 4: simple novelty-aware queue • Layer 5: larger-n and mutation tests • Layer 6: schema filled by hand • Layer 7: weekly review MVP question Can the system produce a conjecture that: • passes at least two checker views, • survives held-out and mutation tests, • and can be translated into a nontrivial substrate constraint? If not, do not scale.   ⸻   Main failure modes in v2 1. Knowledge-base fragility If Layer 0 is inconsistent, every downstream layer becomes noisy. 2. Representation overfitting If normalized forms are poor, the proposer learns artifacts. 3. Checker blindness A narrow checker makes fake survivors. 4. Search collapse The controller overexplores easy families and misses informative ones. 5. Stress weakness Held-out data too similar to training data. 6. Substrate drift Outputs sound physically relevant but do not imply real hardware constraints. 7. Review overload Too many weak survivors consume your scarce attention.   ⸻   Final judgment Your original architecture was already sound. This v2 version makes five key upgrades: • Layer 0 becomes the real center of gravity. • The proposer is split into normalization and conjecture stages. • Checking becomes explicitly multi-view. • Search is prioritized by novelty and substrate relevance, not just passes. • Substrate translation goes through a formal schema before prose. That makes the pipeline much less likely to fool you, and much more likely to produce outputs you can actually use.
